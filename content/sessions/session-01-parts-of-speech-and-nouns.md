# Session 1: Parts Of Speech And Nouns

## Introduction

Hello students, welcome to the first session of the English Mastery Program. Today we are not going to start with difficult grammar rules. We are going to understand the foundation of English.

Many students fear grammar because they think it is only memorisation. But grammar is simply the study of how words work together. Think of a sentence like a cricket team. Every player has a role. In the same way, every word in a sentence has a job.

These jobs are called parts of speech. Today we will understand the 8 parts of speech, and then we will deeply study the first one: nouns.

Think About It:

- Can a sentence exist without words?
- Can a team win if no player knows their role?
- So can English become clear if we do not know the job of each word?

## Parts Of Speech

There are 8 main parts of speech in English.

A noun names a person, place, animal, thing, feeling, or idea. Examples: teacher, Delhi, tiger, book, happiness, freedom.

A pronoun replaces a noun. Instead of saying 'Riya lost Riya's bag', we say 'Riya lost her bag'. Words like he, she, it, they, we, I, you are pronouns.

A verb shows action or state. Run, eat, write, think, is, am, are, was, were are verbs.

An adjective describes a noun. Red bag, tall boy, honest person, beautiful flower.

An adverb describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb. She sings sweetly. He runs fast. They came yesterday.

A preposition shows relation. The book is on the table. The cat is under the chair. She arrived at 5 p.m.

A conjunction joins words or sentences. I like tea and coffee. I studied hard but I was nervous.

An interjection shows sudden feeling. Wow! Alas! Hurray! Ouch!

Example:

Wow! Riya quickly opened the red box on the table.

Identify:

- Wow: interjection
- Riya: noun
- quickly: adverb
- opened: verb
- red: adjective
- box/table: nouns
- on: preposition

Practice:

The clever boy solved the puzzle quickly.

Answer: clever = adjective, boy = noun, solved = verb, puzzle = noun, quickly = adverb.

## What Is A Noun?

A noun is a naming word. If you can name it, it is usually a noun.

A noun can name a person: mother, doctor, student, Rohit.
A noun can name a place: school, park, Mumbai, India.
A noun can name an animal: dog, tiger, elephant.
A noun can name a thing: pen, phone, chair, bottle.
A noun can name a feeling: happiness, fear, anger, sadness.
A noun can name an idea: honesty, freedom, education, bravery.

Many students think nouns are only things we can touch. That is wrong. Can we touch a chair? Yes. Chair is a noun. Can we touch honesty? No. But honesty is still a noun because it names a quality.

Practice:

Find the nouns: Aarav carried his bag to school with happiness.

Answer: Aarav, bag, school, happiness.

## Types Of Nouns

A common noun is a general name: boy, city, river, school, book.

A proper noun is a special name: Aarav, Delhi, Ganga, Delhi Public School, Taj Mahal. Proper nouns always begin with capital letters.

Wrong: he lives in delhi.
Correct: He lives in Delhi.

A collective noun names a group: team, class, crowd, flock, herd, bunch, school of fish.

An abstract noun names something we cannot touch: love, courage, honesty, fear, kindness, childhood.

A material noun names a substance: gold, silver, wood, cotton, rice, water, milk.

Quick Drill:

- city: common
- Mumbai: proper
- team: collective
- honesty: abstract
- gold: material

## Countable And Uncountable Nouns

Countable nouns can be counted: one chair, two chairs, one apple, five apples.

Uncountable nouns cannot be counted directly: water, sugar, rice, furniture, advice, information, bread.

We do not say two waters, three advices, many furnitures, or two breads.

We say:

- a glass of water
- a piece of advice
- a piece of furniture
- a loaf of bread
- a bottle of milk

Correct Forms:

- I need two breads. -> I need two loaves of bread.
- She gave many advices. -> She gave much advice.
- We bought new furnitures. -> We bought new furniture.

## Cursive Practice

Before writing, fix posture, paper position, and pen grip. Sit straight. Keep the paper slightly tilted. Hold the pen lightly. A tight grip makes handwriting shaky.

Practise three strokes: undercurve, overcurve, and slant line. These are the building blocks of cursive.

Class task: one line each of undercurve, overcurve, and slant line.
