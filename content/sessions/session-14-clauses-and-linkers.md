# Session 14: Clauses And Linkers

## Introduction

Today we learn clauses and linkers. This topic makes writing mature and exam-ready.

## Clauses

A clause is a group of words with a subject and a verb.

Main clause can stand alone:
She studied hard.

Subordinate clause cannot stand alone:
because the exam was difficult

Complete sentence:
She studied hard because the exam was difficult.

Types:

Noun clause acts like a noun:
What he said is true.

Adjective clause describes a noun:
The boy who won the prize is my cousin.

Adverb clause gives reason, time, condition, or contrast:
We stayed indoors because it was raining.

## Linkers

Conjunctive adverbs link ideas smoothly:
however, therefore, moreover, furthermore, meanwhile, consequently, nevertheless, for instance, in conclusion.

Addition: moreover, furthermore.
Contrast: however, nevertheless.
Result: therefore, consequently.
Example: for instance.
Summary: in conclusion.

Punctuation:

If a linker joins two complete sentences, use semicolon before it and comma after it.

She studied hard; however, she failed.

If the linker starts a new sentence, use comma after it.

However, she did not give up.

Common Mistake:

Wrong: I studied hard, however I failed.
Correct: I studied hard; however, I failed.

## Practice

1. Join with however: She was tired. She finished homework.
2. Identify clause: This is the house that Jack built.
3. Correct: He works hard, moreover he is honest.
4. Complete: Although the weather was bad, ___.

Answers:

1. She was tired; however, she finished homework.
2. adjective clause
3. He works hard; moreover, he is honest.
4. students' own answer
