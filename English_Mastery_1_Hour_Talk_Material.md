# English Mastery Program: 1-Hour Talk Material For All 20 Sessions

Prepared as teaching material for video classes, live online classes, or classroom teaching.

How to use this file:

- Do not read the script continuously like an essay.
- Teach slowly, write examples on screen or board, ask students to pause and answer, then explain the answer.
- Each session is designed for around 60 minutes using explanation, examples, interaction, practice, speaking, cursive, writing, and homework.
- For videos, keep PPT slides simple and speak around the slides.
- For live classes, ask students to answer orally or in chat every 5 to 7 minutes.

Suggested 1-hour structure for every session:

- 0-5 min: Warm welcome and recap
- 5-30 min: Grammar concept teaching
- 30-40 min: Guided examples and common mistakes
- 40-48 min: Student practice
- 48-53 min: Cursive or handwriting focus
- 53-57 min: Speaking activity
- 57-60 min: Writing task and homework

## Session 1: Parts Of Speech And Nouns

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Hello students, welcome to the first session of the English Mastery Program. Today we are not going to start with difficult grammar rules. We are going to understand the foundation of English.

Many students fear grammar because they think it is only memorisation. But grammar is simply the study of how words work together. Think of a sentence like a cricket team. Every player has a role. In the same way, every word in a sentence has a job.

These jobs are called parts of speech. Today we will understand the 8 parts of speech, and then we will deeply study the first one: nouns."

Ask:

- "Can a sentence exist without words?"
- "Can a team win if no player knows their role?"
- "So can English become clear if we do not know the job of each word?"

### 5-15 Min: Parts Of Speech

"There are 8 main parts of speech in English.

A noun names a person, place, animal, thing, feeling, or idea. Examples: teacher, Delhi, tiger, book, happiness, freedom.

A pronoun replaces a noun. Instead of saying 'Riya lost Riya's bag', we say 'Riya lost her bag'. Words like he, she, it, they, we, I, you are pronouns.

A verb shows action or state. Run, eat, write, think, is, am, are, was, were are verbs.

An adjective describes a noun. Red bag, tall boy, honest person, beautiful flower.

An adverb describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb. She sings sweetly. He runs fast. They came yesterday.

A preposition shows relation. The book is on the table. The cat is under the chair. She arrived at 5 p.m.

A conjunction joins words or sentences. I like tea and coffee. I studied hard but I was nervous.

An interjection shows sudden feeling. Wow! Alas! Hurray! Ouch!"

Board example:

"Wow! Riya quickly opened the red box on the table."

Identify:

- Wow: interjection
- Riya: noun
- quickly: adverb
- opened: verb
- red: adjective
- box/table: nouns
- on: preposition

Ask students to identify parts in:

"The clever boy solved the puzzle quickly."

Answer: clever = adjective, boy = noun, solved = verb, puzzle = noun, quickly = adverb.

### 15-30 Min: What Is A Noun?

"A noun is a naming word. If you can name it, it is usually a noun.

A noun can name a person: mother, doctor, student, Rohit.
A noun can name a place: school, park, Mumbai, India.
A noun can name an animal: dog, tiger, elephant.
A noun can name a thing: pen, phone, chair, bottle.
A noun can name a feeling: happiness, fear, anger, sadness.
A noun can name an idea: honesty, freedom, education, bravery.

Many students think nouns are only things we can touch. That is wrong. Can we touch a chair? Yes. Chair is a noun. Can we touch honesty? No. But honesty is still a noun because it names a quality."

Practice:

"Find the nouns: Aarav carried his bag to school with happiness."

Answer: Aarav, bag, school, happiness.

### 30-40 Min: Types Of Nouns

"A common noun is a general name: boy, city, river, school, book.

A proper noun is a special name: Aarav, Delhi, Ganga, Delhi Public School, Taj Mahal. Proper nouns always begin with capital letters.

Wrong: he lives in delhi.
Correct: He lives in Delhi.

A collective noun names a group: team, class, crowd, flock, herd, bunch, school of fish.

An abstract noun names something we cannot touch: love, courage, honesty, fear, kindness, childhood.

A material noun names a substance: gold, silver, wood, cotton, rice, water, milk."

Quick drill:

- city: common
- Mumbai: proper
- team: collective
- honesty: abstract
- gold: material

### 40-48 Min: Countable And Uncountable Nouns

"Countable nouns can be counted: one chair, two chairs, one apple, five apples.

Uncountable nouns cannot be counted directly: water, sugar, rice, furniture, advice, information, bread.

We do not say two waters, three advices, many furnitures, or two breads.

We say:

- a glass of water
- a piece of advice
- a piece of furniture
- a loaf of bread
- a bottle of milk"

Correct:

- I need two breads. -> I need two loaves of bread.
- She gave many advices. -> She gave much advice.
- We bought new furnitures. -> We bought new furniture.

### 48-53 Min: Cursive Focus

"Before writing, fix posture, paper position, and pen grip. Sit straight. Keep the paper slightly tilted. Hold the pen lightly. A tight grip makes handwriting shaky.

Practise three strokes: undercurve, overcurve, and slant line. These are the building blocks of cursive."

Class task: one line each of undercurve, overcurve, and slant line.

### 53-57 Min: Speaking

"Prepare a 60-second self-introduction. Include your name, class, one hobby, and one goal for this course."

Model:

"My name is Aarav Sharma. I study in Class 9. I enjoy playing cricket and reading short stories. I joined this English course because I want to improve my grammar and speak more confidently."

### 57-60 Min: Writing And Homework

Write a paragraph on "Myself" in 80 to 120 words.

Homework:

- 10 common nouns
- 10 proper nouns
- 5 collective nouns
- 5 abstract nouns
- 5 material nouns
- One paragraph on "Myself"

## Session 2: Pronouns And Verbs

### 0-5 Min: Opening And Recap

"In the last session, we learned that nouns are naming words. Today we will learn what happens when we do not want to repeat the noun again and again. That is where pronouns help. Then we will study verbs, the heartbeat of every sentence."

Recap questions:

- What is a noun?
- Give one proper noun.
- Give one abstract noun.

### 5-20 Min: Pronouns

"A pronoun is a word used in place of a noun. Pronouns make sentences smooth.

Without pronouns: Priya opened Priya's bag and took Priya's book.
With pronouns: Priya opened her bag and took her book.

Subject pronouns do the action: I, you, he, she, it, we, they.
Object pronouns receive the action: me, him, her, us, them.

Example:
He called me.
He is the subject. Me is the object.

Possessive pronouns show ownership: mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs.

Reflexive pronouns end in self or selves: myself, yourself, himself, herself, ourselves, themselves.

Use reflexive pronouns when the subject and object are the same person:
She hurt herself.
I taught myself."

Common mistake:

Wrong: Me and Rohan are going.
Correct: Rohan and I are going.

"Put yourself last and use I when you are the subject."

### 20-35 Min: Verbs

"A verb is a doing or being word. Without a verb, a group of words is not a complete sentence.

Action verbs: run, eat, write, speak, jump.
Being verbs: is, am, are, was, were.
Thinking or feeling verbs: know, believe, love, understand.

Main verb shows the real action.
Helping verb supports the main verb.

Examples:
She is writing a letter.
Writing is the main verb. Is is the helping verb.

They have finished homework.
Finished is the main verb. Have is the helping verb."

Important helping verbs:

is, am, are, was, were, has, have, had, do, does, did, will, shall, can, could, may, might, must, should, would.

### 35-43 Min: Common Mistakes

Wrong: She do homework.
Correct: She does homework.

Wrong: I am knowing the answer.
Correct: I know the answer.

Wrong: Give the pen to Riya and I.
Correct: Give the pen to Riya and me.

"Use the drop-the-other-person trick. Say: Give the pen to me. That sounds correct. So: Give the pen to Riya and me."

### 43-48 Min: Practice

Ask students:

1. Replace with pronouns: Ananya lost Ananya's pen.
2. Find helping and main verb: They have been waiting.
3. Choose: Between you and I/me, this is a secret.
4. Underline verbs: She has eaten lunch and is reading a book.

Answers:

1. Ananya lost her pen.
2. have been = helping, waiting = main
3. me
4. has eaten, is reading

### 48-53 Min: Cursive

"Today we practise wave letters: c, a, d, g, o, q. They all begin with a smooth undercurve. Keep them rounded, not pointed."

Task: 2 lines of c a d g o q, then write "a good cat" five times.

### 53-57 Min: Speaking

Tongue twisters:

- She sells seashells
- Red lorry, yellow lorry
- Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers

Picture talk:

"Look at a picture and describe it using pronouns: He is selling fruit. She is buying vegetables. They are standing near a shop."

### 57-60 Min: Writing

Teach message writing:

"A message is short. It gives important information when someone is absent."

Homework: Write a 50-word message from a phone call received for your mother.

## Session 3: Adjectives, Adverbs, And Degrees

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Today we will learn how to add colour and movement to sentences. Nouns and verbs are the base, but adjectives and adverbs make the sentence clearer and more interesting."

### 5-18 Min: Adjectives

"An adjective describes a noun. It tells us what kind, how many, or which one.

What kind: a red rose, an honest boy, a clever student.
How many: five books, three apples.
Which one: this bag, that chair.

Without adjective: I saw a dog.
With adjective: I saw a small brown dog.

The second sentence gives a clearer picture."

Ask:

"Find adjectives: The tall girl carried a heavy black bag."

Answer: tall, heavy, black.

### 18-30 Min: Adverbs

"An adverb describes a verb. It tells how, when, where, or how often an action happens.

How: She sings sweetly.
When: He came yesterday.
Where: They waited outside.
How often: I always revise my notes.

Many adverbs end in ly: slowly, quickly, softly, carefully. But not all. Fast is already an adverb. We do not say fastly."

Common mistake:

Wrong: He runs fastly.
Correct: He runs fast.

### 30-40 Min: Degrees Of Comparison

"Adjectives have three degrees.

Positive: tall
Comparative: taller
Superlative: tallest

Positive simply describes. Comparative compares two. Superlative compares more than two.

Short adjectives usually take er and est: small, smaller, smallest.
Long adjectives use more and most: beautiful, more beautiful, most beautiful.

Irregular forms:
good, better, best
bad, worse, worst
little, less, least
many/much, more, most"

Common mistakes:

Wrong: more taller
Correct: taller

Wrong: most best
Correct: best

### 40-48 Min: Practice

1. Find adjective and adverb: The clever boy quickly solved the puzzle.
2. Give degrees of intelligent.
3. Correct: My school is more bigger than yours.
4. Fill: Of the two sisters, Meera is the taller/tallest.
5. Arrange: a green big old car.

Answers:

1. clever = adjective, quickly = adverb
2. intelligent, more intelligent, most intelligent
3. My school is bigger than yours.
4. taller
5. a big old green car

### 48-53 Min: Cursive

"Peak letters: i, u, t, s, p, r. Keep the small peaks smooth and even. The letter s should be a curve, not a zigzag."

### 53-57 Min: Speaking

Show and tell:

"Choose one object. Speak for 90 seconds. Use at least 3 adjectives and 2 adverbs."

Model:

"This is my old blue diary. I received it from my sister. I write in it carefully every night."

### 57-60 Min: Writing

Notice writing:

"A notice gives important information to a group. It must answer what, when, where, who, and contact."

Homework: Write a notice for an inter-house debate competition.

## Session 4: Prepositions, Conjunctions, And Interjections

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Today we learn small words that do big work. Prepositions show relation, conjunctions join ideas, and interjections show emotion."

### 5-20 Min: Prepositions

"A preposition shows the relation between two things. It may show place, time, or direction.

Place: in, on, under, beside, between, behind.
Time: at, on, in, before, after, during, since, for.
Direction: to, into, over, across, through.

The book is on the table.
The cat is under the chair.
She arrived at 5 p.m.
We met on Monday.
He was born in 2010."

Time rule:

"Use at for exact time: at 5 p.m.
Use on for days and dates: on Monday, on 5 May.
Use in for months, years, and long periods: in June, in 2026, in the morning."

Fixed phrases:

good at, afraid of, interested in, depend on, proud of, married to.

### 20-35 Min: Conjunctions

"A conjunction joins words, phrases, or sentences.

I like tea and coffee.
I studied hard but I was nervous.
We stayed home because it was raining.

Coordinating conjunctions join equal parts. Remember FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so.

Subordinating conjunctions introduce dependent ideas: because, although, if, when, while, since, until.

Correlative conjunctions work in pairs:
either...or
neither...nor
not only...but also
both...and"

Common mistake:

Wrong: Neither Ravi or Seema came.
Correct: Neither Ravi nor Seema came.

### 35-40 Min: Interjections

"An interjection shows sudden emotion.

Wow! That is beautiful.
Alas! He failed.
Hurray! We won.
Ouch! That hurt.

Use interjections carefully in formal writing. They are common in stories and dialogues."

### 40-48 Min: Practice

1. Fill: She is good ___ drawing.
2. Fill: I was born ___ 2010.
3. Join: It was raining. We went out.
4. Complete: Neither Riya ___ Priya came.
5. Find interjection: Wow! What a goal!

Answers: at, in, although/but, nor, Wow.

### 48-53 Min: Cursive

"Loop letters: l, b, h, k, e, f. Keep loops slim and tall. Practise ll, bb, hh."

### 53-57 Min: Speaking

Read aloud a paragraph. Focus on pauses at commas and full stops.

Peer feedback:

- Was the speed comfortable?
- Were words clear?
- Did the reader pause properly?

### 57-60 Min: Writing

Informal letter:

"An informal letter is friendly and personal. Write to a friend or cousin."

Homework: Write a letter to a cousin describing your summer trip.

## Session 5: Articles And Determiners

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Today we study tiny words that change meaning: a, an, the, some, many, much, few, little. These words come before nouns and help us understand whether something is general, specific, countable, or uncountable."

### 5-22 Min: Articles

"A, an, and the are articles.

Use a before a consonant sound: a boy, a cat, a university. University begins with u but sounds like you, so we say a university.

Use an before a vowel sound: an apple, an orange, an hour, an honest man. Hour and honest begin with h, but h is silent.

Use the when we are talking about something specific.

I saw a dog. The dog was barking.
First mention: a dog. Second mention: the dog.

Use the for unique things: the sun, the moon, the earth.
Use the before rivers, oceans, mountain ranges: the Ganga, the Indian Ocean, the Himalayas.
Use the before superlatives: the tallest, the best."

Zero article:

"Do not use the before most country names, languages, and subjects.

India is beautiful.
I speak Hindi.
Mathematics is difficult.

Wrong: the India
Correct: India"

### 22-35 Min: Determiners

"Determiners come before nouns and tell how much, how many, which one, or whose.

Examples: this, that, these, those, my, your, some, any, many, much, few, little, each, every.

Use many with countable nouns: many books, many students.
Use much with uncountable nouns: much water, much money.

Use few with countable nouns and little with uncountable nouns.

A few means some and sounds positive.
Few means almost none and sounds negative.

I have a few friends. That means I have some friends.
I have few friends. That means almost no friends.

A little means some.
Little means almost none."

### 35-43 Min: Common Mistakes

Wrong: He is a honest man.
Correct: He is an honest man.

Wrong: Delhi is the capital of the India.
Correct: Delhi is the capital of India.

Wrong: I have much books.
Correct: I have many books.

Wrong: He is best player.
Correct: He is the best player.

### 43-48 Min: Practice

1. ___ MBA from ___ IIM is ___ dream.
2. I have ___ questions to ask. (few/a few)
3. ___ French is ___ beautiful language.
4. There is ___ hope left, so do not give up. (little/a little)

Answers:

1. An MBA from an IIM is a dream.
2. a few
3. French is a beautiful language.
4. a little

### 48-53 Min: Cursive

"Tail letters: g, j, p, q, y, z. The tail goes below the baseline. Keep the tail gentle, not sharp."

### 53-57 Min: Speaking

JAM: Just A Minute.

Topics:

- My favourite season
- If I had one superpower
- My ideal Sunday

Rule: speak for 60 seconds without long pauses.

### 57-60 Min: Writing

Formal letter:

"A formal letter is polite, official, and direct. It has sender address, date, receiver address, subject, salutation, body, and closing."

Homework: Write a formal letter to the principal requesting permission for a school event.

## Session 6: Simple Present, Simple Past, And Simple Future

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Today we begin tenses. Tense tells us time: now, before, or later. The simple tenses are the foundation."

### 5-18 Min: Simple Present

"Simple present is used for habits, facts, routines, and universal truths.

I play cricket every day.
She plays piano.
The sun rises in the east.

With I, you, we, they, use base verb: I go, they play.
With he, she, it, add s or es: he goes, she plays, it works.

Signal words: daily, every day, always, usually, often, sometimes, never."

Common mistake:

Wrong: She go to school.
Correct: She goes to school.

### 18-30 Min: Simple Past

"Simple past is used for completed actions in the past.

I visited my grandmother yesterday.
We played football last week.
Gandhi led the Dandi March in 1930.

Regular verbs add ed: walk, walked; play, played.
Irregular verbs change: go, went; eat, ate; see, saw."

Signal words: yesterday, last week, ago, in 2015, when I was young.

Common mistake:

Wrong: I goed to the market.
Correct: I went to the market.

### 30-38 Min: Simple Future

"Simple future uses will plus base verb.

I will call you tomorrow.
She will complete homework.
They will visit us next week.

After will, do not use to.

Wrong: I will to call.
Correct: I will call."

Signal words: tomorrow, next week, soon, later, in a few days.

### 38-48 Min: Practice

1. My brother ___ to college every morning. (go)
2. Change to past: She writes a letter.
3. Change to future: They play cricket.
4. Identify tense: The Ganga flows through Varanasi.
5. Correct: He don't likes spicy food.

Answers:

1. goes
2. She wrote a letter.
3. They will play cricket.
4. simple present
5. He does not like spicy food.

### 48-53 Min: Cursive

"Uppercase A to M. Capital letters are taller and clearer. Practise names: Aarav, Bengaluru, Diwali."

### 53-57 Min: Speaking

JAM topics:

- Why every student should play a sport
- The best book I have read
- What I would change about my school

### 57-60 Min: Writing

Email writing:

"An email needs To, Subject, greeting, short body, and sign-off."

Homework: Write an email requesting two days' leave.

## Session 7: Continuous And Perfect Tenses

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Last session we studied simple tenses. Today we learn continuous tenses and perfect tenses. Continuous shows ongoing action. Perfect shows completed action connected to another time."

### 5-22 Min: Continuous Tenses

"Present continuous: am/is/are + verb-ing.
I am reading. She is writing. They are playing.
Use for actions happening now.

Past continuous: was/were + verb-ing.
I was reading. They were playing.
Use for actions going on at a past time.

Future continuous: will be + verb-ing.
I will be studying at 8 p.m."

Signals:

- now, at this moment, currently
- while, as, at 8 o'clock yesterday
- at this time tomorrow

Common mistake:

Wrong: I am knowing the answer.
Correct: I know the answer.

"Know, believe, love, understand, and want are stative verbs. They are usually not used in continuous form."

### 22-38 Min: Perfect Tenses

"Present perfect: has/have + V3.
I have finished my homework.
She has gone to the market.

Past perfect: had + V3.
The train had left before we arrived.

Future perfect: will have + V3.
By next year, I will have completed my course."

Teach V1, V2, V3:

go, went, gone
eat, ate, eaten
write, wrote, written
see, saw, seen

Since vs for:

"Since gives starting point: since Monday, since 2020.
For gives duration: for two hours, for ten years."

### 38-48 Min: Practice

1. Change: She writes a letter. -> present continuous.
2. Fill: I ___ three movies this week. (see)
3. Identify: The movie had started before we arrived.
4. Correct: He has left yesterday.
5. Fill: They ___ for the bus when it rained. (wait)

Answers:

1. She is writing a letter.
2. have seen
3. past perfect
4. He left yesterday.
5. were waiting

### 48-53 Min: Cursive

"Uppercase N to Z. Practise your full name ten times in cursive."

### 53-57 Min: Speaking

Storytelling:

"Tell a 3-minute story using past tenses. Include one suspense line like: Suddenly, the lights went out."

### 57-60 Min: Writing

Diary entry:

"A diary is personal. Mention what happened, how you felt, and what you learned."

Homework: Write a diary entry about a memorable day.

## Session 8: Perfect Continuous And 12 Tense Revision

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Today we complete all 12 tenses. Perfect continuous tenses show an action plus its duration. They answer: for how long?"

### 5-22 Min: Perfect Continuous Tenses

"Present perfect continuous: has/have been + verb-ing.
I have been studying for two hours.
She has been learning English since April.

Past perfect continuous: had been + verb-ing.
They had been waiting for an hour before the bus arrived.

Future perfect continuous: will have been + verb-ing.
By June, I will have been learning English for one year."

Signals:

- for
- since
- how long
- by

### 22-35 Min: Perfect Vs Perfect Continuous

"Use perfect when result matters.
I have written three letters. The result is three completed letters.

Use perfect continuous when duration matters.
I have been writing letters all morning. The activity and time are important."

Common mistakes:

Wrong: I am studying since morning.
Correct: I have been studying since morning.

Wrong: She has been knowing him for years.
Correct: She has known him for years.

### 35-43 Min: 12 Tense Map

"There are 3 times: present, past, future.
There are 4 aspects: simple, continuous, perfect, perfect continuous.
3 x 4 = 12 tenses."

Use one verb: write.

- I write.
- I am writing.
- I have written.
- I have been writing.
- I wrote.
- I was writing.
- I had written.
- I had been writing.
- I will write.
- I will be writing.
- I will have written.
- I will have been writing.

### 43-48 Min: Practice

1. We ___ for you for an hour. (wait)
2. Identify: She had been crying for twenty minutes.
3. Correct: I am painting since morning.
4. Fill: I have lived here ___ 2018 and ___ six years.

Answers:

1. have been waiting
2. past perfect continuous
3. I have been painting since morning.
4. since, for

### 48-53 Min: Cursive

"Spacing drill. Letter gaps should be equal. Word gaps should be wider. Write: cursive, homework, Bengaluru, elephant."

### 53-57 Min: Speaking

Extempore:

"30 seconds preparation, 2 minutes speaking."

Topics:

- Should students use smartphones in class?
- What makes a good friend?
- Online learning vs classroom learning

### 57-60 Min: Writing

Descriptive paragraph:

"Use the five senses: sight, sound, smell, taste, touch."

Homework: Describe a market, railway station, beach, or school playground.

## Session 9: Subject-Verb Agreement

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Today we learn concord, which means agreement. The subject and verb must match."

### 5-22 Min: Basic Agreement

"Singular subject takes singular verb.
The boy plays.
The girl sings.

Plural subject takes plural verb.
The boys play.
The girls sing.

In simple present, singular verbs often end with s, but plural verbs do not."

Explain:

"The s jumps. One boy plays. Many boys play."

### 22-38 Min: Important Rules

"Two subjects joined by and usually take plural verb:
Ravi and Seema are here.

But one idea takes singular:
Bread and butter is my breakfast.

With either...or and neither...nor, the verb agrees with the nearer subject:
Neither the captain nor the players are ready.
Neither the players nor the captain is ready.

Each, every, everyone, somebody, nobody take singular verb:
Everyone is invited.
Each of the boys is present.

One of the + plural noun takes singular verb:
One of the books is missing.

Some nouns look plural but are singular:
news, mathematics, physics.

Some nouns look singular but are plural:
police, cattle.

Paired items are plural:
scissors, trousers, spectacles."

### 38-43 Min: Common Mistakes

Wrong: Each of the boys are present.
Correct: Each of the boys is present.

Wrong: My trousers is new.
Correct: My trousers are new.

Wrong: The box of chocolates are on the table.
Correct: The box of chocolates is on the table.

"Ignore the words in between. The subject is box, not chocolates."

### 43-48 Min: Practice

1. One of my friends ___ in Canada. (live)
2. Physics ___ difficult. (be)
3. The box of pens ___ on the shelf. (is/are)
4. Correct: The furniture are old.

Answers:

1. lives
2. is
3. is
4. The furniture is old.

### 48-53 Min: Cursive

"Copy 5 short sentences in cursive. Keep all words evenly spaced."

### 53-57 Min: Speaking

Role play: shopkeeper and customer.

Use comparison:

- Is this cheaper?
- Do you have a better model?
- Can you reduce the price?

### 57-60 Min: Writing

Descriptive essay on a place.

Homework: Write 200 words describing a place you visited.

## Session 10: Active And Passive Voice

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Voice tells us what the sentence focuses on: the doer or the receiver. Active voice focuses on the doer. Passive voice focuses on the action or receiver."

### 5-20 Min: Active Vs Passive

"Active: Meera broke the window.
Passive: The window was broken by Meera.

Active pattern: Subject + Verb + Object.
Passive pattern: Object + be verb + V3 + by + Subject."

Steps:

1. Find subject, verb, object.
2. Move object to the front.
3. Add correct be verb.
4. Change main verb to V3.
5. Add by plus doer if needed.

### 20-38 Min: Passive Forms

"Simple present:
Active: The chef cooks meals.
Passive: Meals are cooked by the chef.

Simple past:
Active: Shakespeare wrote Hamlet.
Passive: Hamlet was written by Shakespeare.

Present perfect:
Active: Someone has stolen my bicycle.
Passive: My bicycle has been stolen.

Continuous:
Active: The teacher is explaining the chapter.
Passive: The chapter is being explained by the teacher.

Modal:
Active: You must obey the rules.
Passive: The rules must be obeyed."

## Session 11: Reported Speech - Statements

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Reported speech means telling someone what another person said, without using the exact words."

### 5-22 Min: Direct To Indirect

"Direct: Ravi said, 'I am hungry.'
Indirect: Ravi said that he was hungry.

Three things change:

1. Pronouns
2. Tense
3. Time and place words"

Pronouns:

"I changes according to the speaker. You changes according to the listener."

Tense backshift:

- am/is/are -> was/were
- present simple -> past simple
- past simple -> past perfect
- has/have -> had
- will -> would
- can -> could
- may -> might

Time words:

- now -> then
- today -> that day
- tomorrow -> the next day
- yesterday -> the previous day
- here -> there
- this -> that

### 22-38 Min: Examples

She said, "I love my school."
She said that she loved her school.

He said, "I am studying now."
He said that he was studying then.

Ravi said, "I will call you tomorrow."
Ravi said that he would call me the next day.

Mother said, "I have cooked dinner."
Mother said that she had cooked dinner.

Teacher said, "Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius."
Teacher said that water boils at 100 degrees Celsius.

"Universal truths do not change."

### 38-43 Min: Common Mistakes

Wrong: He said that he will come tomorrow.
Correct: He said that he would come the next day.

Wrong: She said that I am tired.
Correct: She said that she was tired.

### 43-48 Min: Practice

2. The guard said, "The gate closes at 10 p.m."
3. Rohan said to me, "I saw your brother yesterday."

Answers:

2. The guard said that the gate closed at 10 p.m.
3. Rohan told me that he had seen my brother the previous day.

## Session 12: Reported Speech - Questions, Commands, Requests

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Last session we reported statements. Today we report questions, commands, requests, advice, and exclamations."

### 5-20 Min: Questions

"Yes/no questions use asked if or asked whether.

Direct: He said, 'Are you coming?'
Indirect: He asked if I was coming.

WH questions keep the WH word:
Direct: She said, 'Where do you live?'
Indirect: She asked where I lived.

Important: reported questions do not use question word order.

Wrong: He asked where do I live.
Correct: He asked where I lived."

### 20-35 Min: Commands, Requests, Advice

"Commands use told or ordered + to + V1.

The officer said, 'Stop.'
The officer ordered me to stop.

Requests use requested + object + to + V1.

She said, 'Please help me.'
She requested me to help her.

Advice uses advised + object + to + V1.

Mother said, 'Drink warm water.'
Mother advised me to drink warm water.

Negative commands use not to + V1.

He said, 'Do not shout.'
He told me not to shout."

### 35-43 Min: Exclamations

"Exclamations become statements with exclaimed joyfully, exclaimed sorrowfully, or exclaimed with surprise.

Direct: 'Hurray! We won,' they said.
Indirect: They exclaimed joyfully that they had won.

Direct: 'Alas! I failed,' he said.
Indirect: He exclaimed sorrowfully that he had failed."

## Session 13: Modals

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Today we study modals. Modals are helping verbs that add meaning like ability, permission, possibility, advice, request, or obligation."

### 5-25 Min: Modal Meanings

"Can shows ability or informal permission:
She can swim. Can I borrow your pen?

Could shows past ability or polite request:
He could run fast as a child. Could you help me?

May shows formal permission or possibility:
May I come in? It may rain.

Might shows weak possibility:
He might be at the library.

Must shows strong obligation or strong guess:
You must wear a helmet. She must be tired.

Should shows advice:
You should revise daily.

Would shows polite request or imagined situation:
Would you like tea? I would travel if I had money."

### 25-38 Min: Modal Grammar Rule

"After a modal, always use base verb.

Correct: He can swim.
Wrong: He can to swim.

Correct: You must attend.
Wrong: You must to attend.

Correct: I would like to join.
Wrong: I would liked to join."

Certainty ladder:

- must = almost sure
- should = likely
- may/might/could = possible

Permission:

"In exams and formal situations, use may for permission."

### 38-48 Min: Practice

2. You ___ wear a seatbelt. It is the law.
3. Correct: He can to swim.
4. Correct: When she was young, she can speak French.

1. Could/Would
2. must
3. He can swim.
4. When she was young, she could speak French.

## Session 14: Clauses And Linkers

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Today we learn clauses and linkers. This topic makes writing mature and exam-ready."

### 5-20 Min: Clauses

"A clause is a group of words with a subject and a verb.

Main clause can stand alone:
She studied hard.

Subordinate clause cannot stand alone:
because the exam was difficult

Complete sentence:
She studied hard because the exam was difficult."

Types:

"Noun clause acts like a noun:
What he said is true.

Adjective clause describes a noun:
The boy who won the prize is my cousin.

Adverb clause gives reason, time, condition, or contrast:
We stayed indoors because it was raining."

### 20-38 Min: Linkers

"Conjunctive adverbs link ideas smoothly:
however, therefore, moreover, furthermore, meanwhile, consequently, nevertheless, for instance, in conclusion.

Addition: moreover, furthermore.
Contrast: however, nevertheless.
Result: therefore, consequently.
Example: for instance.
Summary: in conclusion."

Punctuation:

"If a linker joins two complete sentences, use semicolon before it and comma after it.

She studied hard; however, she failed.

If the linker starts a new sentence, use comma after it.

However, she did not give up."

Common mistake:

Wrong: I studied hard, however I failed.
Correct: I studied hard; however, I failed.

### 38-48 Min: Practice

1. Join with however: She was tired. She finished homework.
2. Identify clause: This is the house that Jack built.
3. Correct: He works hard, moreover he is honest.
4. Complete: Although the weather was bad, ___.

Answers:

1. She was tired; however, she finished homework.
2. adjective clause
3. He works hard; moreover, he is honest.
4. students' own answer

## Session 15: Conditionals

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"A conditional sentence has an if clause and a result clause. It tells what happens if something else happens."

### 5-30 Min: Five Types

"Zero conditional is for general truths:
If you heat water, it boils.
Pattern: if + present, present.

First conditional is for real future possibility:
If it rains, we will cancel the picnic.
Pattern: if + present, will + V1.

Second conditional is for unreal present or imagination:
If I had a million rupees, I would buy a farm.
Pattern: if + past, would + V1.

Third conditional is for unreal past or regret:
If I had studied harder, I would have passed.
Pattern: if + had + V3, would have + V3.

Mixed conditional connects past condition to present result:
If I had learnt French, I would speak it today."

### 30-40 Min: Common Mistakes

Wrong: If I will see him, I will tell him.
Correct: If I see him, I will tell him.

"Do not use will in the if clause of first conditional."

Wrong: If I would have studied, I would have passed.
Correct: If I had studied, I would have passed.

Wrong: If I was rich, I would buy a car.
Formal: If I were rich, I would buy a car.

### 40-48 Min: Practice

Answers:

1. were, would make
2. third conditional
3. falls
4. If I get time, I will call you.

## Session 16: Sentence Transformation

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Sentence transformation means changing the form of a sentence without changing the meaning. The golden rule is: meaning must remain the same."

### 5-25 Min: Transformation Types

"Affirmative to negative:
He is honest.
He is not dishonest.

Assertive to interrogative:
Everyone loves success.
Who does not love success?

Assertive to exclamatory:
The view is very beautiful.
How beautiful the view is!

Simple to compound:
Getting up early, he went out.
He got up early and went out.

Simple to complex:
Being tired, she slept.
Because she was tired, she slept.

Compound to complex:
He is rich but unhappy.
Though he is rich, he is unhappy."

### 25-38 Min: Sentence Types

"A simple sentence has one main clause.
Example: He went home.

A compound sentence has two main clauses joined by and, but, or, so.
Example: He was tired, but he worked.

A complex sentence has one main clause and one subordinate clause.
Example: Although he was tired, he worked."

Degree transformation:

"Ravi is taller than Mohan.
Mohan is not as tall as Ravi."

## Session 17: Editing, Omission, And Jumbled Sentences

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Today is exam technique day. Editing, omission, and jumbled sentences are scoring areas if you know the method."

### 5-20 Min: Editing

"In editing, each line has one error. Common errors:

- tense
- subject-verb agreement
- preposition
- article
- pronoun
- spelling

Read the full sentence first. Then check slowly."

Examples:

She go to school. -> She goes to school.
My sister don't like tea. -> My sister doesn't like tea.
The book on the table are mine. -> The book on the table is mine.

### 20-32 Min: Omission

"In omission, a word is missing. Missing words are usually small: a, an, the, is, are, of, to, and, but.

Example:
He is going ^ market.
Missing: to/the
Correct: He is going to the market.

He is one ^ best students.
Missing: of/the
Correct: He is one of the best students."

"Use the S-V-O-P-T skeleton:
Subject + Verb + Object + Place + Time.

Words: won / the / yesterday / we / match
Sentence: We won the match yesterday.

Find the subject first, then verb, then object, then details."

## Session 18: Vocabulary - Idioms, Phrasal Verbs, One-Word Substitution

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Vocabulary makes language powerful. Today we learn three tools: idioms, phrasal verbs, and one-word substitutions."

### 5-20 Min: Idioms

"An idiom is a phrase whose meaning is different from the literal words.

Piece of cake: very easy.
The exam was a piece of cake.

Spill the beans: reveal a secret.
Do not spill the beans about the surprise party.

Under the weather: unwell.
I am feeling under the weather.

Bite the bullet: face a difficult situation.
He had to bite the bullet and apologise.

Blessing in disguise: something bad that becomes good.
Missing the bus was a blessing in disguise because I met my old friend."

### 20-35 Min: Phrasal Verbs

"A phrasal verb is verb plus particle.

Give up: quit.
Do not give up.

Look after: care for.
Please look after my dog.

Put off: postpone.
The meeting was put off.

Call off: cancel.
The match was called off.

Turn down: reject.
He turned down the offer.

Find out: discover.
I found out the truth."

Common mistake:

Wrong: He gave up from smoking.
Correct: He gave up smoking.

### 35-43 Min: One-Word Substitution

"One-word substitution replaces a long phrase with one precise word.

One who does not eat meat: vegetarian.
One who cannot read or write: illiterate.
A person who walks on foot: pedestrian.
A person who studies stars: astronomer.
Government by the people: democracy.
A place where books are kept: library.
A person who knows many languages: polyglot."

## Session 19: Punctuation And Full Revision Drill

### 0-5 Min: Opening

"Punctuation marks are traffic signals for writing. Without them, sentences become confusing."

### 5-25 Min: Punctuation Marks

"Full stop ends a statement:
I finished my work.

Question mark ends a question:
Where are you going?

Exclamation mark shows strong feeling:
What a beautiful day!

Comma separates items or pauses:
We bought apples, oranges, and bananas.

Apostrophe shows contraction or possession:
It's raining. Riya's book.

Quotation marks show direct speech:
She said, 'I will come.'

Colon introduces a list:
He needs three things: patience, courage, and honesty.

Semicolon joins related independent clauses:
She studied hard; however, she failed."

### 25-38 Min: Capitalisation And Common Errors

Wrong: My father, is a doctor.
Correct: My father is a doctor.

Do not put a comma between a simple subject and verb."

"It's means it is. Its means belonging to it.
It's raining. The dog wagged its tail."
